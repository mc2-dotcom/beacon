Two State-Adjacent Newsrooms,
One Region, Five Charts.

Al Arabiya and Al Jazeera report the same wars and the same elections — but their lexicons, their topic mixes, and their engagement footprints diverge in ways that map almost exactly onto Saudi and Qatari state interests. This dashboard turns 405,000 tweets, several thousand articles, and 14 editorial dimensions into five interactive charts.

Al Arabiya English uses "militant" 839 times; Al Jazeera English uses it 4 times. Conversely, AJ English uses "genocide" 575 times and AJ+ uses it 859 times. Al Arabiya's 153 "genocide" mentions are almost always inside attributed quotes, not editorial voice.

The "leak point"

AJ+'s 10.2% "genocide" density and −1.7 Trump sentiment mark it as the system's most overtly editorial voice. The news site is the system's most restrained. The X account sits in between by design.

Why this is structural

Format dictates voice. Articles carry institutional reputational risk; flagship social accounts allow more latitude; youth-oriented sub-brands (AJ+) operate more like commentary outlets.

Section 08 · Ownership Fingerprints

Three signatures that map directly onto state interests.

Saudi signal · Brotherhood

Al Arabiya Arabic's 883 Brotherhood tweets vs. Al Jazeera Arabic's 19 (a 46:1 ratio) tracks the 2014 Saudi designation of the Muslim Brotherhood as a terrorist organization. AJ's mechanism is omission, not promotion: it protects the Brotherhood by not subjecting it to the negative coverage AA produces.

Verified
Qatar signal · self-coverage

Across all topics analyzed in AJ English's article database, Qatar is the only subject receiving a net-positive sentiment score (+0.09). Every other topic — Israel, Iran, Trump, the U.S., Hamas, Hezbollah — scores negative.

Strong
Saudi signal · Iran

Al Arabiya Arabic's estimated −0.97 sentiment on Iran is the most extreme score in the dataset for any outlet on any topic. 40,596 Iran tweets and 1,701 uses of "إرهاب" (terrorism) constitute a sustained editorial campaign.

Strong
Domestic vs. international voice

Both outlets moderate for Western audiences. The AJ Arabic ↔ AJ English martyrs/genocide swap is the cleanest example of conscious code-switching for a Western readership.

Verified

Section 10 · Methodology & Limitations

Confidence levels & how to read this report.

Sentiment scoring uses a five-point scale: +2 / +1 / 0 / −1 / −2. Scores are analyst-estimated based on lexical analysis, framing patterns, and representative samples. Lexical counts are drawn from keyword searches across the full tweet corpus and independently verified against the raw data.

Evidence TierThresholdWhere it appears
VerifiedIndependently confirmed against primary data — exact or near-exact matchLexical counts, top-post engagement, follower/volume figures
StrongDirectionally confirmed with minor numerical varianceCross-cohort sentiment averages, framing-pattern findings
ModerateAnalytically sound but based on estimated scores or limited samplesSub-brand sentiment scores, cross-channel inferences
Low confidenceThin data; findings are suggestive, not conclusiveAbraham Accords & Brotherhood for AJ Eng X (<15 tweets each)

Key Limitations

  1. Al Arabiya news articles are not in the same pre-scored database as AJ Eng articles. AA news scores carry MODERATE confidence vs. HIGH for AJ Eng news.
  2. No Arabic-language news articles were systematically analyzed for either outlet. Arabic analysis relies entirely on X/Twitter data.
  3. Only five flagship accounts are monitored. No journalist accounts, no AJ Investigates handle, no AA Business sub-brand.
  4. Abraham Accords & Muslim Brotherhood have very thin AJ social data (<15 tweets each). Findings on those dimensions for AJ's English social voice are LOW CONFIDENCE.
  5. AJ Arabic tweets have zero semantic embeddings in the database; Arabic searches are keyword-only, which may undercount topically relevant tweets.
  6. The study window (Apr 2024 – Apr 2026) is unusually conflict-heavy; baseline sentiment may be more negative than a non-conflict period.
