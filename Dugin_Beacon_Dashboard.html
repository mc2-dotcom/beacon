Russian political philosopher Alexander Dugin — architect of “Neo-Eurasianism” and the “Fourth Political Theory” — has become an increasingly visible vector for antisemitism, anti-Israel delegitimization, and anti-democratic ideology entering American public discourse. Section 02 · Ideological Foundations

Three interlocking pillars — one anti-Western, anti-democratic, and antisemitic project.

Pillar I: Neo-Eurasianism

Drawing on the interwar Russian émigré tradition (Trubetskoy, Savitsky), Dugin frames Russia as the heart of a distinct “Eurasian” civilization destined to lead a global coalition against Anglo-American hegemony. His 1997 Foundations of Geopolitics articulated this as a strategic program: a Moscow–Berlin–Paris axis, alliance with Iran, and the destabilization of the U.S. through “separatism and ethnic, social and racial conflicts.”

Verified — Stanford/Hoover (Dunlop)
Pillar II: The Fourth Political Theory

In his 2009 book of the same name, Dugin proposed a “fourth” ideology to supersede liberalism, communism, and fascism — all of which he treats as products of Western modernity and rejects. In practice the framework borrows from all three while positioning liberalism as the primary enemy. In the 1990s he called for “a genuine, true, radically revolutionary and consistent fascist fascism” in Russia.

60 Minutes + academic
Pillar III: Traditionalism

Dugin self-identifies as a follower of René Guénon and Julius Evola — thinkers who rejected Enlightenment modernity in favor of a perennial sacred order. In his own words: “Atheism, pluralism, progressism, Enlightenment — all that things are totally, totally evil.” Evola’s racial-civilizational hierarchy explicitly placed Jews in a subordinate spiritual category; Dugin’s synthesis treats liberalism, secularism, and human rights as demonic forces to be destroyed.

Verified self-identification
The Atlanticist / Eurasianist binary places Israel on the enemy side

Dugin divides the world between Eurasianism (land-based, traditional, sacred, Russia-led) and Atlanticism (sea-based, liberal, secular, U.S./U.K.-led). Israel and Zionism are placed firmly on the Atlanticist side. In a March 2026 tweet he made the alignment explicit: those “on the side of God (Palestine, Russia, Iran, China, the Resistance)” versus those “on the side of devil (Baal, Epstein, globalism, Dajjal, Antichrist).”

Verified — @agdugin

Three framings to deploy when Dugin or Duginist ideas are cited approvingly.

Framing III · DEMOCRATIC

“Dugin is not a conservative — he’s an anti-democratic ideologue who has praised fascism, rejected pluralism as ‘totally evil,’ and founded a party that combined Soviet and Nazi symbolism. The American conservative tradition is built on individual liberty, constitutional governance, and democratic self-rule — everything Dugin explicitly opposes.”

How to choose which framing to use

SECURITY frames best for national-defense, foreign-policy, and Republican-leaning audiences who value American strategic interests. MORAL frames best for Jewish institutional, faith-based, and human-rights audiences. DEMOCRATIC frames best for cross-ideological audiences, civic groups, and conservatives who would reject explicitly anti-democratic positioning if shown clearly.

Section 10 · Methodology and Limitations

Sources, evidence tiers, and how to read the citations.

This dossier draws on four categories of sources: (1) Dugin’s own published writings, social-media posts, and media appearances (primary sources); (2) academic analyses by leading scholars of Russian nationalism, including Marlène Laruelle, John Dunlop, Andreas Umland, and Anton Shekhovtsov; (3) institutional reports from ADL, U.S. State Department, SPLC, and MEMRI; and (4) social-media monitoring data from X/Twitter and podcast platforms. Throughout we distinguish Dugin’s own words (in quotation marks with source), close associates’ characterizations (identified by name), and secondary analysis (attributed to the analyst or organization).

Limitations to flag
